# UniCart Design Context

## Design Context

### Users
Young, fashion-aware consumers (18–35) who shop across Zara and Uniqlo. They're comfortable with tech, browse multiple retailers, and want a single place to compare and track items before buying. Context of use: usually casual browsing sessions, both on desktop and while switching between tabs. They expect apps to feel polished and current — they notice when something looks dated.

### Brand Personality
**Clean · Bold · Editorial**

UniCart sits at the intersection of a productivity tool and a fashion product. It should feel like something a designer would use — confident, intentional, and slightly elevated. Not sterile or corporate. Warm intelligence over cold utility.

Emotional goals: users should feel organized and in control, with a quiet sense of delight. Like the app *gets* fashion.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Light mode only.**

References:
- **Stripe** — crisp, high-contrast type; confident use of whitespace; subtle depth via shadow and color; nothing wasted
- **Notion** — structured yet breathable layouts; type hierarchy that guides the eye naturally; functional elegance
- **Glossier** — editorial image treatment; soft but intentional color accents; youthful and fashion-forward without being loud

Anti-references:
- Monochrome-only designs (no personality, cold)
- Boring default system fonts (Arial, Helvetica with no weight variation)
- Generic "SaaS gray" dashboards
- Heavy drop shadows or gradients that feel Web 2.0

**Typography:** Use a font with personality — a geometric or humanist sans with strong weight contrast (e.g. Inter with bold display weights, or a custom display pairing). Headlines should be bold and editorial. Body text should be clean and highly legible.

**Color:** Move beyond the current all-gray palette. Introduce a warm neutral base (e.g. warm off-white `#faf9f7` instead of cold `#fafafa`) and one intentional accent color — could be a muted terracotta, slate blue, or sage — used sparingly for interactive elements and highlights, not decoration.

**Cards:** Product image cards should feel like a fashion lookbook — tall aspect ratios, clean typography below, generous padding. Imagery is the hero.

**Spacing:** Generous. Breathable. Don't compress content.

**Interactions:** Subtle, fast transitions. No heavy animations. Hover states should feel responsive but not theatrical.

### Design Principles

1. **Image first** — Product photography is the hero of every card. Give images room to breathe. Never crop awkwardly.
2. **Type with intent** — Every text element should have a clear hierarchy role. Use weight and size contrast boldly; avoid same-weight walls of text.
3. **Accent, don't decorate** — Color exists to direct attention (interactive states, key data) — not to fill space. One accent color, used with restraint.
4. **Editorial confidence** — Layouts should feel considered, not generated. Spacing, alignment, and grid choices should reflect a design eye.
5. **Functional warmth** — The app should feel like a well-designed product people enjoy using, not a utility they tolerate. Small details (empty states, transitions, microcopy) contribute to this.
